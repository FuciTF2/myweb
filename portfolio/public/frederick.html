PSYCHOLOGICAL & OPERATIONAL PROFILE — FULL NARRATIVE ASSESSMENT

⚠ Subject status: DECEASED — CONFIRMED. Cause of death: ████████████████. File retained for ongoing operational record.

§ ASSESSMENT OVERVIEW

Frederick David isn't defined by coldness or rigidity.

He's defined by something much simpler.

Confidence.

Not arrogance. Not ego. A quiet, unshakable belief that he knows what he's doing. And most of the time — he's right.

§ PHASE I — ORIGIN PRE-BERKSLEY

DAVID was not loud about who he was. He didn't need to be. Pre-program assessment identified a subject with an unusually stable self-concept — not the product of privilege or insulation, but of a genuine, tested understanding of his own capabilities.

SELF-KNOWLEDGE Demonstrated clear and accurate understanding of his abilities before the program began. Neither overestimated nor underestimated his limits.
DECISIVENESS Trusted his decisions without excessive deliberation. In high-pressure environments this reads as calm. It is, in fact, something more fundamental.
COMPOSURE Did not second-guess himself under duress. Where others broke or bent, DAVID simply performed. Consistently. Without apparent effort.

In a place like BERKSLEY, that kind of mindset stands out. It also, in ways that would only become clear later, survives intact.

§ BERKSLEY — WHERE CONFIDENCE BECOMES IDENTITY NOTABLE

The BERKSLEY program did not strip DAVID down. Where it eroded other subjects — removing fear, removing hesitation, removing the parts of a person that interfere with function — it encountered in DAVID something with different properties.

It reinforced him.

PRECISION Already high. Improved further under program conditions. Assessment: near-exceptional. No upper plateau identified during testing.
REACTION Sharpened to a degree that moved beyond training into something more reflexive. Decisions made at speed with accuracy that others achieved only through conscious effort.
INSTINCT Near-perfect operational instinct. Not recklessness. The opposite. An understanding of situations that outpaced formal analysis.

He wasn't surviving the program by chance. He was surviving because he was good. And he knew it. This is not arrogance. Arrogance overstates. DAVID was, in most assessable respects, stating facts.

§ OPERATIONAL ROLE — THE PILOT WHO DOESN'T MISS

Flying was not merely an assigned function. It was where DAVID was, in every measurable sense, at his best.

There is a particular quality to someone who knows they will not make a mistake. It reads as calm. It projects as stillness. It creates, in those around them, a sense that the situation is more controlled than it may actually be.

DAVID had this quality. In the air, it was total.

No panic. No hesitation. Just execution. Assessors noted that DAVID's presence in an aircraft changed the behavioural profile of other personnel aboard. He made people steady without saying a word.

§ PERSONALITY PROFILE — QUIETLY ASSURED

DAVID did not boast. Did not argue unless necessary. Did not lose control under any documented circumstances. In a unit composed of personalities that were, by design, unstable — DAVID was the exception.

COMPOSURE Maintained across all documented scenarios. Including scenarios specifically designed to break it.
RELIABILITY Consistent to a degree that became predictable. Assessors noted this as both an asset and, in retrospect, a vulnerability.
DANGER The steadiest person in a room is often the most dangerous. They don't broadcast. They don't warn you. They simply act.
§ POST-BERKSLEY — TRANSITION FIELD INTELLIGENCE

When the BERKSLEY unit dissolved, DAVID did not dissolve with it. Where others fractured or disappeared into the noise, DAVID transitioned with the same unhurried competence he brought to everything.

He became a smuggler. And it fit him perfectly.

HIGH-RISK FLIGHT Functionally identical to what he'd always done. The aircraft changed. The requirements didn't.
PRECISION NAV Routes designed to avoid detection. Low altitude. Tight windows. Exactly the conditions where DAVID performed best.
SPLIT-SECOND CALLS Controlled danger rather than chaos. A distinction DAVID understood and others in his profession typically didn't.

He also, it appears, believed something important: that the past was behind him. Not forgotten. Contained. Handled the same way he handled everything else. With control. With confidence. Filed and moved on from.

He was wrong about that. But he didn't know it yet.

§ TERMINATION — BROOK APPROACH INCIDENT REPORT

BROOK did not approach DAVID the way he approached the others. There was no confrontation. No announcement. No opportunity to apply the skills that had kept DAVID alive and ahead for this long.

BROOK studied him. Understood him. And then did something DAVID did not expect:

METHOD — CONFIRMED

A charge placed on the rear rotor assembly. Not during combat. Not in opposition. In the middle of what DAVID did best. In the air. Where he was untouchable.


BROOK did not try to beat DAVID at what DAVID was good at. He removed the conditions that made DAVID's competence relevant.

The helicopter went down. And with it, the illusion — however quietly held — that skill was sufficient protection against what was coming.

For the first time, skill wasn't enough. Control was compromised. The outcome wasn't his to dictate.

— Post-incident assessment, DAVID, F.

§ FINAL CONFRONTATION — SKILL VS INEVITABILITY

When BROOK located him after the crash, the dynamic was unlike the confrontations that preceded it.

DAVID was not chaotic like DUKKEBRIE. Not quietly anchoring like KAPLERI. He was still, in every observable way, himself. Injured. Composed. Aware of what had happened and what was about to.

No panic. Just recognition.

The most controlled man in the unit — meeting the one outcome he could not control.

§ LEGACY — WHAT DAVID REPRESENTS
Mastery — that was real, earned, and genuinely exceptional. And still not enough.
Control — as identity. The belief that competence is armour. It isn't.
Confidence — built on accuracy. Undone not by failure — but by something outside the system it relied on.
Frederick never doubted his ability to stay in control —
until the moment the sky itself
stopped listening.

You found this by clicking a name in a roster on a page that wasn't supposed to exist.
Frederick would have respected the precision.
