Bullet & Spite
The Story Behind the Shooting

Origins

Nobody remembers who started the war. That's the thing about wars that go on long enough — the reason gets buried under the wreckage, and all that's left is the habit of fighting. The sector known as the Spite Corridor had been contested for so long that maps stopped marking it as belonging to anyone. It just existed. Hot, loud, and full of things trying to kill you.

The pilot had no name on record. Call sign: Bullet. Assigned to a ship that wasn't supposed to exist, flying missions that were never logged. The kind of operator you send when you need something done and don't want to know how.

The Corridor

The Spite Corridor got its name from the engineers who were told to build a supply route through it. They said it couldn't be done. Command said do it anyway. The engineers did it out of spite, half-expecting it to fail. It didn't. It became the most valuable stretch of space in the sector, which meant it immediately became the most fought-over.

By the time Bullet was assigned there, the Corridor had changed hands eleven times. Nobody was counting anymore. The enemies didn't have a flag either — just ships, just guns, just the same stubborn refusal to give ground.

Why It Matters

Bullet wasn't fighting for the Corridor. Bullet wasn't fighting for Command. Somewhere in the third run through enemy formations, with the ship half on fire and the radio cut out, it stopped being about orders. It became something simpler and harder to explain — the kind of thing you only understand when you're in it.

You keep going because stopping feels worse. You fire because it's the only honest thing left to do. Bullet and spite. That's all there ever was.
