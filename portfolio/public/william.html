§ ASSESSMENT OVERVIEW

William D. Brook is not a hero. Not quite a villain.

He is the end result of a process.

§ PHASE I — ORIGIN PRE-BERKSLEY

Before anything else, BROOK was a child trying to survive in a dying world. Ascana provided the education that no institution could — or would.

HUNGER Normalised early. Not experienced as emergency — experienced as state.
DEATH Common. Proximate. Ceased to register as aberration by age ██.
TRUST Identified early as a liability. Behaviour adapted accordingly.

By the time BERKSLEY found him, the foundations were already in place. The program did not create BROOK's survival instinct.

They weaponised it.

§ PHASE II — THE BREAKING POINT CRITICAL EVENT

There is no single clean moment where BROOK changes. Assessors who look for one are looking for the wrong thing.

The transformation is gradual. Quiet. Almost unnoticeable from the outside — which is precisely what makes it complete.

DEFINING INCIDENT — TRIAL PHASE 3

A child forced to kill another child in order to survive. Not out of anger. Not hatred. Not ideology.

Necessity.

From this point forward, death stops being shocking. It becomes practical.

Psychological assessment conducted 72 hours post-incident noted no observable distress. No grief response. No elevated cortisol markers. The relevant pathways had already begun their reorganisation. The process was, from the program's perspective, proceeding correctly.

§ PHASE III — TRANSFORMATION BERKSLEY OUTPUT

BERKSLEY refines what remains. The program does not build from nothing — it strips away what interferes and sharpens what survives.

CHEMICAL Fear response suppressed. Physiologically incapable of standard threat reaction.
CONDITIONING Hesitation removed at the operational level. Response time: exceptional.
DEPLOYMENT Missions reinforce compliance. Outcome dependency replaces independent will.

The result: precise, efficient, reliable. BROOK saves lives as a medic. Takes them as a sniper. There is a strange balance to it — but field assessors should not mistake function for morality. They are not the same thing in this subject.

§ PHASE IV — THE ILLUSION OF BROTHERHOOD

Among the BERKSLEY survivors, something almost human forms. Not friendship in the recognisable sense. Something closer to:

SHARED SUFFERING Experienced the same trials. Carry the same damage. No explanation required between them.
MUTUAL UNDERSTANDING Each knows what the others are. No pretence necessary. Functions as a form of honesty.
COLLECTIVE IDENTITY Not alone in what they've become. For BROOK, this is the closest available approximation of belonging.

This does not fix him. Nothing fixes him. But it gives him something to stand on. For a while.

§ PHASE V — THE BETRAYAL TERMINATION EVENT

When BRIGGS, S. releases his hand, it is not merely an act of betrayal. It is confirmation.

Everything BROOK was. Everything he did. Everything he survived. Meant nothing.

He was not a soldier. Not a person. He was an experiment that had reached its conclusion. And he was being discarded.

"This was your final mission. Thank you for your service, William."

— BRIGGS, S. — final recorded communication prior to termination attempt

What makes this moment significant is not the fall. It is what the fall confirms. Every suspicion BROOK ever had about what he was to this program — every moment he chose not to examine too closely — lands with him.

PRESUMED DEAD
§ PHASE VI — WHAT COMES BACK

BROOK survives. This is not in question.

What is less clear — and what this assessment attempts to address — is the psychological state of what survives.

There is no anger in the traditional sense. No grief. No visible rage. No breakdown. No recovery arc.

What field operatives have reported instead is something harder to categorise:

A quiet, unwavering shift. A decision made without ceremony, without announcement, without apparent emotional content. He simply begins moving in a direction. And does not stop.

§ CURRENT STATUS — 1997

BROOK moves through the world like something unfinished. Speaks little. Reacts less. Takes contracts. Draws no attention to himself beyond the work.

But everything he does points in one direction. Each target is not just a job. It is a step. Toward what, he has not articulated. He does not need to.

HE REMEMBERS All of it. Without distortion. Without the mercy of forgetting.
HE CONTINUES Not because he believes in the outcome. Because he chose a direction and has not yet finished.
HE DOES NOT STOP Stopping would leave him alone with what's left of himself.
§ PSYCHOLOGICAL CORE — FINAL ASSESSMENT

At his core, BROOK is a contradiction that does not resolve and shows no signs of resolving.

A man incapable of fear — who lives entirely in the shadow of everything he has lost.
A survivor — who does not value his own survival.
A weapon — that is fully aware it was used.
William D. Brook is not chasing revenge to feel something again.
He is finishing it because it is the only thing left
that still makes sense.
